1 · Why this assessment exists

Courses age fast in AI. Without a feedback loop, content drifts away from what students actually need.

A static curriculum, however well designed, is a snapshot. The pace of AI tooling, the framings learners arrive with, and the patterns instructors notice in the room all change quarter over quarter. The library has been re-architected so it can refresh itself: external trend signal flows in from public feeds, performance signal flows in from cohort feedback, and both pair against the canonical course packages to inform editorial decisions.

The premise

Three loops, one library

Generate, Evaluate, Distribute, Observe — the CDLC. Until last week the Observe loop was the missing piece: we shipped content into the world but did not bring its performance back in. With the feedback pipeline live, the loop closes.

The certifications no longer have to wait for an annual revision. A module can be flagged, ticketed, and revised inside a single cohort cycle.

What this doc is

The synthesis layer above the digests

Two digests already exist as machine-friendly artifacts: outputs/trends/latest.md for external signal and outputs/feedback/latest.md for cohort signal. They are detailed and stable.

This assessment is the executive-readable layer above them. It pulls the headline numbers, threads them against the canonical packages, and produces specific decisions a course owner can act on this week.

5 · Cross-cutting patterns

What we learn from looking at the whole portfolio

Five signals show up only when the seven certifications are read as a portfolio, not as seven independent products. The first one is the loudest the data has ever produced.

Pattern 1 · Market signal · Loudest this week

Every non-AI cert is asking for AI-overlay content

PMM students and instructor: AI-product launch examples. PLC students and instructor: 2026 AI-strategy lens. PMC students: AI-product metrics. DPM students: cross-link to AI eval stack. Four certs, four cohorts, all unprompted, all in the same week.

This is not an isolated request. It is the single highest-frequency editorial signal the feedback pipeline has ever surfaced. Senior PMs do not want a separate AI track; they want their existing cert with an AI overlay.

Recommendation: ship an "AI overlay" content layer that reuses the existing AI atom catalog (Reliability Contract, Five AI Value Archetypes, AI Eval Stack, Confidence UX, AI PRD) and threads it into the relevant module of every non-AI cert. A single atom can serve three certs. This is the highest-leverage move in the portfolio this quarter.

Pattern 2 · Sequencing

Module 3 is where everything goes wrong

Three of seven certs flagged this week, and all three on the same module: AI Vibe Coding M3, PMC M3, DPM M3. Three different content domains (eval scaffolding, stakeholder management, feature stores) showing the same failure shape.

Hypothesis: M3 is systematically asked to ship the heaviest technical or organisational content while still introducing learners to the cert's central artifact. The pacing fails the same way regardless of subject.

Recommendation: a portfolio-level M3 pacing audit before next cohort. Reallocate dense content into M2 and M4. Test in one cert first.

Pattern 3 · Atoms

Throughline atoms travel further than any single cert

juno-pm-throughline, ai-product-management-execution-arc, ai-product-strategy-six-questions appear in every module of their certs. Vibe Coding's Confidence Line plays the same role. DPM's "pipeline-as-product" plays the same role this week.

Students consistently cite these throughlines as the single most useful piece of the cert. Instructors describe them as the spine that keeps a session navigable.

Recommendation: promote "throughline" as a first-class atom category. Treat them as portfolio-level assets. Test reuse across certs (e.g. a "Decision-line" throughline derived from PMC M1's discovery framing).

Pattern 4 · Trust as a portfolio theme

Three AI M4s are teaching the same lesson

AI Vibe Coding M4 (close the trust gaps), AI PM M4 (AI-native UX, hallucination + black-box + control gaps), AI PS M4 (the contract — reliability, golden datasets, confidence UX) are three framings of one underlying skill: making the AI product trustworthy enough to ship. Today the cross-link does not exist in any deck.

The Pattern 1 signal extends this: PMM and PLC students are asking for trust language they can use in launches and board conversations. The Trust Layer is not just a course atom — it is a portfolio asset.

Recommendation: a shared "Trust Layer" atom set referenced from the three AI M4s, PMM M3, and PLC M2. Single source of truth.

Pattern 5 · Library coverage

Tracked-only certs outnumber canonical certs

Three certs are canonical (full packages in library/packages/certifications/). Four are tracked-only (feedback flows in, no canonical content). For four of seven products, this assessment can recommend signals but cannot point at a specific atom or module to revise.

The instinct to package these immediately should be tempered: PMC and PLC are mature programs with existing content artifacts elsewhere. The right move is to atomize first, not to recreate. Pull out the strongest reusable atoms (PMC M1 discovery framing, PLC M1 vision frameworks, PMM M2 positioning rewrite, DPM M1 pipeline-as-product) and bring those into the library; full packages can follow.

Recommendation: a focused atom-extraction sprint targeting the four tracked-only certs. Two atoms per cert in eight weeks.

Pattern 6 · Feedback asymmetry

Rich student feedback. Thinner instructor reflections. No completion data.

This week's 39 records: 28 student, 7 instructor, 4 cohort. Useful balance, but the instructor leg is still under-instrumented for the breadth we are now tracking. Cohort-level metrics lag a week. Post-cert artifact submissions are not in the corpus at all.

Recommendation: instrument the missing legs. See section 6 below.

7 · Instrumentation gaps

What we still cannot measure

The feedback pipeline closed the obvious gap (we can now see what students and instructors think across the full portfolio, week over week). Seven gaps remain. Each one constrains what this assessment can confidently say.

Gap 1

Time-on-slide / time-on-module

We can see NPS but not engagement. We do not know if students stay on a deck or skim. Closing this gap would tell us whether low NPS is content-quality or pacing-related, which would have changed this week's M3 recommendations.

Gap 2

Post-cert artifact submissions

The certifications all promise a forked GitHub repo as the deliverable. We do not currently track whether that repo exists, what is in it, or how often it is updated. The artifact is the proof of learning — that is the metric that should govern the portfolio.

Gap 3

Cohort-to-cohort longitudinal NPS

We can compare this week to the trailing 4-week mean, but we cannot confidently track a single cohort's trajectory across the cert. Closing this gap requires consistent cohort_id across every survey response.

Gap 4

Instructor pairing performance

The same module taught by two different instructor pairs almost certainly produces different NPS. We do not yet attribute feedback to instructor combos. Closing this gap would let us learn from our best teaching pairs, not just our best content.

Gap 5

External-trend to cert-relevance overlay

The trends digest tells us what is rising in the market. The feedback digest tells us how our content is landing. We do not yet automatically join the two: which rising market phrases have no atom mapping in our library?

Gap 6

Enterprise-vs-consumer split

Enterprise workshops will repackage the same atoms with a different audience. Today our feedback corpus is consumer-cohort-only. As enterprise pilots come online, the same comment ("too dense") will mean very different things in those rooms.

Gap 7 · Surfaced this week

Cross-cert NPS comparability

The seven certs span very different audiences (early-career PMs in PMC, senior leaders in PLC, marketers in PMM, AI-curious PMs across the AI portfolio). Comparing W22 NPS 9.33 (AI PS) to NPS 7.17 (PMC) without controlling for audience baseline is misleading; the audiences are not the same. Today the digest treats them as comparable.

Closing this gap requires either per-audience baselines (NPS minus the cert's own historical mean) or audience metadata on the cohort. The fix is small once decided.
